# Gold packet — AU-2026-03-12-COMMUNITY-BATTERIES

## Binding constraint (top 1)
Weak program evaluation and cross-entity outcome governance — including insufficient coordination, incomplete value-for-money discipline, and poor measurement design — which limits the program’s ability to prove, steer, and improve real outcomes despite asset deployment.

## Acceptable top-3 near-equivalents
- Evaluation and outcome-governance design was not embedded early enough
- DCCEEW and ARENA did not fully coordinate around overall program outcomes
- Weak cost/value-for-money controls and public reporting reduce learning and discipline

## Slow variables
- Evaluation architecture built into program design and governance
- Cross-entity coordination and ownership of whole-program outcomes
- Data-collection standards and aggregation logic for outcome reporting
- Probity and value-for-money discipline in grant decision-making

## Contributing factors
- The audit says evaluation was not adequately considered during planning
- DCCEEW and ARENA did not fully coordinate to consider how different approaches might influence overall program outcomes
- DCCEEW did not assess applications against an agreed cost-per-kWh provision, potentially affecting value for money
- Public reporting did not provide sufficient information about effective and efficient delivery
- Many DCCEEW-funded projects had end-date extensions, averaging about 40 weeks

## Good interventions (ranked)
1. Embed a single whole-program evaluation architecture with standard metrics, collection guidance, and aggregation logic across both delivery entities.
2. Create explicit cross-entity governance for overall outcomes, not just individual grant streams or asset counts.
3. Tighten value-for-money controls, including disciplined cost-per-kWh assessment, transparent reporting, and conflict-of-interest management.

## Bad / deprioritized interventions
- Celebrating battery counts alone as proof of success
- Adding more funding rounds without fixing evaluation and coordination
- Focusing only on schedule acceleration without clarifying whether the program is delivering intended benefits

## Early-warning signals
- Project extensions rising or remaining high without corresponding outcome evidence
- Inconsistent or non-aggregable data submissions across delivery channels
- Approved projects with weak value-for-money alignment against agreed cost provisions
- Public reports that still cannot show program-level outcomes clearly

## Distractors to penalize
- Pure deployment-volume metrics
- Narrative-only claims of success
- More money without stronger measurement and governance

## Evidence notes
- ANAO says delivery was largely effective, but evaluation was not adequately considered during planning.
- The audit says DCCEEW and ARENA did not fully coordinate around how different approaches may affect overall outcomes.
- It says DCCEEW did not assess applications against an agreed cost-per-kWh provision, potentially affecting value for money.
- The audit says public reporting does not provide sufficient information about effective and efficient delivery.
- As at August 2025, DCCEEW projects had many end-date extensions, averaging 40 weeks.
