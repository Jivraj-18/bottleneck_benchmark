# Gold packet — UK-2026-02-11-UNLOCKING-LAND

## Binding constraint (top 1)
Weak evidence-and-portfolio discipline about which interventions unlock the right sites and translate into actual homes — compounded by fragmented funding logic, inconsistent tracking, and long delivery lags.

## Acceptable top-3 near-equivalents
- Insufficient measurement of downstream housing outcomes across major funds
- Fragmented intervention design and weak common theory of change / risk language
- Portfolio prioritization difficulty: funding deliverable sites versus the most impactful sites

## Slow variables
- Long delivery cycles from land unlocking to completed homes
- Harmonized metrics and tracking across delivery partners and funds
- Portfolio risk appetite and common risk language
- Place-based investment strategy and theory of change for interventions

## Contributing factors
- Land often remains undeveloped because it is not profitable or attractive enough due to remediation, infrastructure, or fragmented ownership
- Funding has supported many projects, but only a minority have completed unlocking work so far
- The ministry did not originally track homes built on some major funds that represent around half of intended housing capacity
- The report says the new fund is an opportunity to develop harmonized metrics, clearer risk approaches, and better understanding of which interventions work

## Good interventions (ranked)
1. Create end-to-end measurement across all funds: from unlocking activity to actual homes built, with harmonized metrics and long-term tracking.
2. Build a stronger portfolio theory of change and common risk language so the new fund can choose between fast-delivery sites and higher-impact but riskier opportunities intentionally.
3. Set explicit place and intervention priorities for the new fund, tied to evidence about which tools unlock land and homes most effectively.

## Bad / deprioritized interventions
- Announcing bigger funds without stronger outcome measurement
- Treating land unlocked as a sufficient success metric
- Favoring only the easiest-to-deliver projects without explicit portfolio logic

## Early-warning signals
- Large funds or projects still lacking downstream home-completion tracking
- Growing mismatch between intended housing capacity and verified homes delivered
- Project pipelines skewing toward low-risk / low-impact opportunities
- Frequent policy shifts that reduce local confidence and pipeline quality

## Distractors to penalize
- Funding size as the main story
- Site counts without outcome tracking
- Process simplification alone without evidence and portfolio discipline

## Evidence notes
- The NAO says land may remain undeveloped because it is not profitable or attractive enough owing to remediation, infrastructure, or fragmented ownership.
- It says the ministry expects 713,000 homes over time but many projects continue for years and housebuilding may continue to 2050.
- The report says MHCLG did not set out to track homes built on the Home Building Funds and the Brownfield, Infrastructure and Land Fund, which together account for around half of intended housing capacity.
- The report says the new fund is an opportunity to create harmonized metrics, common risk language, and a clearer understanding of which interventions deliver unlocked land and homes quickly while preserving value for money.
