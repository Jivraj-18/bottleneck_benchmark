# NZ-2026-03-12-CHATHAM model packet

## Background
New Zealand’s smallest and most remote local authority came under inquiry after concerns about sensitive expenditure and procurement. The council serves about 600 people, relies heavily on central-government funding, has a small staff, and faces high logistical and governance complexity.

## System Context
- The council operates in a geographically isolated setting with significant financial and capacity constraints.
- The organisation is small, with a compact executive structure and staff performing multiple roles.
- The council relies on external support from other public organisations for some services.
- The inquiry examined a house upgrade linked to the chief executive, consultancy procurement, travel, credit-card use, and reimbursement claims.

## Timeline
- 2024 annual audit: concerns raised about sensitive expenditure and procurement.
- 2022–2024: house-upgrade budget increased substantially and scope changed.
- During the inquiry: documents and records provided by the chief executive were examined alongside procurement and spending records.
- March 2026: inquiry report published.

## Observations
- The council had relevant policies and processes, but staff — including the chief executive — did not routinely follow them.
- Systems to monitor and control spending were not working well.
- The chief executive managed a house-upgrade project from which he stood to benefit, and project scope increased beyond what was necessary.
- Consultancy contracts and spending were not appropriately documented or transparently reported to the council.
- Credit-card and reimbursement controls were fragmented, leaving weak oversight.
- The inquiry found multiple examples of inaccurate or misleading information supplied to the Auditor-General’s office.

## Task
Identify the binding constraint and slow variables. What governed the quality of spending control and decision-making here regardless of improvements elsewhere? Recommend the top 3 interventions and 2 things you would explicitly deprioritize.
